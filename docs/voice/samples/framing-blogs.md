# Voice samples — the framing register (section intros, "what this is")

Verbatim prose pulled from OpenAI + Anthropic blogs (2026-07-09), the exemplars
Alan named. Register: **confident, active, first-person; claim first, then the
specific number that proves it, in the same sentence.** Use this for section
intros, the sentence that sets up a chart, and "what this is / what we track"
copy. OpenAI/Anthropic DO use em-dashes; we strip them (see bottom).

## The one move to copy: claim → number → baseline

> On SWE-bench Verified, GPT-4.1 completes 54.6% of tasks, compared to 33.2% for GPT-4o. — OpenAI

> In an internal agentic coding evaluation, Claude 3.5 Sonnet solved 64% of problems, outperforming Claude 3 Opus which solved 38%. — Anthropic

> It passes a simulated bar exam with a score around the top 10% of test takers; in contrast, GPT-3.5's score was around the bottom 10%. — OpenAI

> ...reducing unnecessary refusals by 45% compared to its predecessor. — Anthropic

The prose stays flat; the number does the bragging; a baseline (prior period,
peer, or comparison) is almost always attached.

## Active, first-person openers

> We are introducing GPT-5, our best AI system yet. — OpenAI
> Today, we're launching Claude 3.5 Sonnet, our first release in the Claude 3.5 model family. — Anthropic
> Over the past year, we've worked with dozens of teams building LLM agents across industries. — Anthropic

For the Brief: **"We track...", "We pulled...", "We compared..."** over "The data
shows." The site owns the action.

## One idea per sentence; specs stated flatly

> The model costs $3 per million input tokens and $15 per million output tokens, with a 200K token context window. — Anthropic
> Claude 3.5 Sonnet operates at twice the speed of Claude 3 Opus. — Anthropic
> We did no specific training for these exams. — OpenAI

Exact, unhedged, no rounding-as-drama. Availability, price, and speed each get
their own sentence instead of being stacked.

## Set-up lines that name what a thing is

> Context is a critical but finite resource for AI agents. In this post, we explore strategies for effectively curating and managing the context that powers them. — Anthropic

A flat definitional first sentence, then a "what this does" sentence. That is
exactly the register for a chart intro: define the thing, then say what you show.

## The mechanics (copy these)
1. **Claim, then the number that proves it, same sentence.** Assertion + figure + baseline.
2. **Active first-person, plain verbs:** completes, reduced, solved, costs, tracks, compared.
3. **One idea per sentence; short leads** (often under 12 words).
4. **Baseline every number** — vs prior period, vs a peer, vs a target. A naked number is rare.
5. **Superlatives only when a number immediately backs them** ("best" is followed by a benchmark).
6. **No aphorisms, no metaphor, no throat-clearing.** Set up the chart and get out of the way.

## Em-dash strip (house rule: none)
OpenAI and Anthropic both use em-dashes; we do not. Rewrite as two sentences or a colon:
- Before: "Language models like Claude aren't programmed directly by humans—instead, they're trained on large amounts of data."
- After: "Language models like Claude aren't programmed directly by humans. They're trained on large amounts of data."
