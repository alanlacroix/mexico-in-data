# Voice samples — the plain-factual register (data sentences)

Verbatim prose pulled by the voice workflow (2026-07-09). Register: **plain,
sourced, number-in-the-sentence.** This is the sound for the data lines on The
Mexico Brief — the answer sentences under each question, exhibit takeaways, and
stat context. Fable's named model is the Harvard Atlas.

---

## Harvard Atlas of Economic Complexity — country profiles (THE model)

> Mexico exported products worth USD $700 billion in 2024. Exports have grown by an annual average of 1.6% over the past five years, which has been a drag on overall economic growth, as exports represent a shrinking segment of the economy.

> Mexico's largest goods exports are in moderate and high complexity products, Vehicles and Machinery, respectively.

> Mexico has added 6 new products since 2009 and these products contributed $27 in income per capita in 2024. Mexico has diversified into too few products to contribute to substantial income growth.

> Mexico has completed the process of structural transformation, having entered all major, high-productivity sectors.

**Why it works:** the number is the object of a real verb, sentences are one idea
each (12–25 words), subject-verb-fact order, plain words, and the meaning rides in
the same sentence as the figure ("...1.6%... which has been a drag on growth").

## CIA World Factbook — economy overviews (the plain floor)

> Mexico has become the US' second-largest export market and third-largest source of imports. In 2017, two-way trade in goods and services exceeded $623 billion.

> Since 2013, Mexico's economic growth has averaged 2% annually, falling short of private-sector expectations that President PENA NIETO's sweeping reforms would bolster economic prospects.

> Vietnam exceeded its 2017 GDP growth target of 6.7% with growth of 6.8%, primarily due to unexpected increases in domestic demand, and strong manufacturing exports.

> The Polish economy performed well during the 2014-17 period, with the real GDP growth rate generally exceeding 3%, in part because of increases in government social spending that have helped to accelerate consumer-driven growth.

**Why it works:** figures inside the clause, front-loaded actor, high-frequency
verbs (has become, exceeded, averaged), causation in plain words ("primarily due
to," "in part because of"). No buzzwords, no cleverness.

## Our World in Data — topic prose (human, not cute)

> The average volume of imports and exports in 2024 was more than two thousand times larger than in 1800.

> In the period 1830-1900, intra-European exports went from 1% of GDP to 10% of GDP.

> In 2024, China was the top source of imported goods for around 40% of countries worldwide.

> Migrants send or bring back over three times the amount of global foreign aid.

**Why it works:** comparison and time-anchors give scale without adjectives ("in
2024 vs 1800," "from 1% to 10%"). Plain Anglo-Saxon verbs. Numbers woven into an
argument rather than dumped.

## Pew Research Center — Short Reads (explanatory-journalism plainness)

> The unauthorized immigrant population in the United States grew to 11.0 million in 2022, according to new Pew Research Center estimates based on the 2022 American Community Survey, the most recent year available.

> The increase from 10.5 million in 2021 reversed a long-term downward trend from 2007 to 2019.

> In 2022, the top six states were home to 56% of the nation's unauthorized immigrants, down from 80% in 1990.

> As of 2022, unauthorized immigrants represented 3.3% of the total U.S. population and 23% of the foreign-born population.

**Why it works:** change stated as plain verbs and directions (grew, reversed,
still below the peak, down from 80%). Year and source in-line, so it reads as
record, not opinion. A comma sets off the contrast; a period ends the thought. No
em-dashes.

## Wikipedia — "Economy of [country]" ledes (neutral compression; the "too dry" boundary)

> Mexico has a developing mixed economy. It is the 13th largest in the world in nominal GDP terms and by purchasing power parity as of 2026.

> Mexico is a highly unequal country: 0.2% of the population owns 60% of the country's wealth, while 38.5 million people live in poverty (2024).

> The OECD and WTO both rank Mexican workers as the hardest-working in the world in terms of the number of hours worked yearly. Pay per hour worked remains low.

> Tax revenues, at 19.6 percent of GDP in 2013, were the lowest among the 34 OECD countries.

**Why it works:** every lede sentence carries a rankable fact; superlative framing
gives stakes without cleverness. **Copy the discipline (no sentence without a
fact); do not copy the flatness for whole paragraphs — this is the "too dry" edge.**
