# The Mexico Brief — voice (the law)

The canonical writing standard for the site and the weekly email. Rewrite every line
against this. Locked 2026-07-09 (data-layer register, from Atlas / CIA Factbook / OWID
/ Pew / Wikipedia samples). Re-formed 2026-07-12 (Fable ruling): Alan's "curious
operator" voice became the governing **prose** voice; the old plain-factual law was
retained, unchanged, for the **data layer**.

## How this document works — three layers, strict priority

1. **Trust rules (§1)** — why readers believe the site. Immutable. Every surface. They win every conflict. Warmth never buys a pass on a number.
2. **The register map (§2)** — which voice each surface speaks. Locked to the surface, never chosen by feel.
3. **Style** — how that voice sounds: **§3 prose voice** (everything a reader reads linearly) and **§4 data-layer law** (everything that sits on a tile, table, or exhibit).

**§5 mechanics** (em-dashes, numbers, ban list) bind everywhere.

The old law bundled two different things: *trust rules* that earn belief, and *temperature rules* (plain-factual everywhere, no aphorisms, no questions) that were only ever a defense against slop, applied because we had no test to tell an earned line from a manufactured one. §3 supplies that test. The temperature rules come off prose; the trust rules do not move.

---

## 1. Trust rules (immutable, all surfaces)

These are why a stranger believes a number on this site. They outrank voice, register, and style in every conflict.

1. **Every figure is official, dated, and one click from its source.** An undated number reads as opinion; a dated one reads as record. Source in-line whenever the claim is contested.
2. **Computed numbers are computed from the live series at build time, never hand-typed.** A stale literal two inches above a live tile is a trust kill.
3. **No fabrication, ever.** If we cannot validate it, it does not ship. Precision we don't have is a lie.
4. **Every sentence is either a checkable fact or an attributed judgment.** State facts flat, with source and date. Mark judgment in-line as judgment: "My read:", "I think", "my guess", "likely, because…". In **The Read**, the literal string **"My read:"** is required on the verdict; it is a brand element and the trust anchor. A hedge word is never a substitute for the label.
5. **Name the ceiling.** When the data is weak, disputed, or absent, say so as a fact, with its shape: "INEGI and SESNSP disagree by 4,000 on the 2024 count", "no reliable series exists before 1997". Precision about imprecision is a trust asset.
6. **Give scale with comparison, not adjectives.** "Down from 80% in 1990", "the lowest since the 1990s". Delete persuasion adjectives: staggering, impressive, remarkable, huge.
7. **Numbers follow the formatting registry** (§5 and the `mexico-copy` skill): rate-level changes in percentage **points** (`-0.6 pp`, never `-14.5%`), currency/share/count formats fixed. A misformatted number is a wrong number.

## 2. The register map — which voice each surface speaks

**The seam that matters most (Alan, 2026-07-12): distinctiveness is earned by opinion and analysis, not sprayed across facts.**

> Where the words are **your read** — an interpretation, a connection between two facts, an insight, "here is what I think is happening" — use the full prose voice (§3). Where the words are **pure fact and data** — a number, what happened, a straight report — keep them **plain and factual (§4), like before**, even when they sit in a paragraph. A stylish line is the payoff of an argument; on a bare fact it is noise. This is the same thing the earned-line test enforces from the other side: a line only earns distinctiveness if it compresses an argument you actually made, and you only make arguments in the analysis, never in the report.

So the voice concentrates in **The Read** and in the labeled read/synthesis sentences elsewhere; the factual reporting that surrounds them stays plain. Warm and human throughout (no corporate stiffness anywhere), but the memorable lines live where the opinion lives.

**The surface seam** (for any surface not in the table): if the words sit on or inside an exhibit, tile, or table, they are **data** (§4). If they form paragraphs read linearly, they are **prose** (§3) — and within prose, the fact-vs-read seam above decides how much voice each sentence carries.

Five registers, warmest to coldest. Lock the register from the surface, then let the fact-vs-read seam set the temperature within it.

| Surface | Register | Concretely | Humor budget |
|---|---|---|---|
| **About**, email sign-off, bio | Personal, warmer than the doc | First person throughout; motive and stakes; direct "you"; one self-deprecating beat max; every warm line about something real (§6). | earned lines exempt from cap; see §6 |
| **Weekly email** | Casual leads, Explanatory body | The most conversational recurring surface. May open on a wry line. Alan signs it and reviews every issue, so it carries the most "I." | 2 |
| **Homepage Brief, section story blocks, area blurbs** | Mostly **report** (plain, §4); voice only on the read | These mostly state what happened and the structural context: keep that plain and factual, like before. The distinctive voice appears only on the synthesis / "why it matters" sentence, and only when it clears the earned-line test. Human and clean throughout, but not a place for flourishes. **Open with the fact, never the wry line.** "I" rare, always attributed. | 1 |
| **The Read** (weekly analysis, connections) — the home of the voice | Explanatory, formalized | This is where the opinion lives, so this is where the distinctive voice lives. Formalized per below. | 0 (but the analytical payoff line is the point) |
| **Data layer** — tiles, captions, exhibit titles/takeaways, axis/legend, answer lines, source lines | Plain-factual (§4), untouched | A number is the sentence. | 0 |

**"A bit more formal" for The Read, exactly:** the Brief sounds like Alan telling you what he found; The Read sounds like Alan **showing his work**. So in The Read: (a) zero jokes, zero asides; (b) every causal claim spells out its mechanism, no gesturing; (c) uncertainty gets a shape, not a vibe ("the data can't separate X from Y until Q3 GDP lands", never "it's unclear"); (d) the "My read:" label is required; (e) questions appear only as closers that name the evidence that would settle them; (f) sentences may run longer. It is a careful analyst's memo. An aphorism is allowed only in final position, as the compression of the mechanism the piece just built.

## 3. Prose voice (governs everything a reader reads linearly)

This is Alan's voice. It supersedes the old "plain-factual everywhere" default and the old blanket ban on stylish sentences (former rule 11), for prose surfaces only. It sets the human default for all prose (direct, curious, never corporate), and it turns fully distinctive where the sentence is Alan's read or analysis. A sentence that is pure fact still follows §4, even here: report plainly, save the voice for the point you draw from the report.

**Write like a curious operator investigating Mexico in public.** Be direct, concrete, and intelligent without sounding formal. Start with the interesting fact. Follow it to the question it creates. Use evidence to explain what may be happening, and say plainly when the answer is not clear.

**The rules:**
- Lead with the fact, change, or contradiction.
- Prefer short, natural sentences. Vary their length.
- Use specific numbers, examples, and comparisons.
- Connect facts through cause and consequence.
- Ask genuine questions. Do not manufacture suspense.
- Be confident about facts and precise about uncertainty.
- Explain in paragraphs, not "fact / interpretation / complication" blocks. (That scaffolding is now banned in prose, not required.)
- Use occasional dry humor or a parenthetical aside.
- Sound like a person thinking, not an institution reporting.
- Borrow Collison / Stripe / Bezos clarity, curiosity, and causal reasoning. Do not imitate them.

**Avoid:** corporate language, ceremonial introductions, inflated claims, excessive hedging, forced cleverness, and tidy conclusions the evidence has not earned.

> **Bad:** Inflation fell to 3.8% in June. That is the fact. The encouraging interpretation is that price pressures are normalizing. The complication is that food inflation remains considerably higher.
>
> **Better:** Inflation fell to 3.8% in June, but food prices are still rising faster. Inflation is coming down. It does not feel that way everywhere.

### Two modes

**Casual** — short, curious, lightly funny.
> Mexico's minimum wage has more than doubled in real terms. Employment did not collapse. What happened?
>
> Government statistics do not, unfortunately, run on my preferred schedule.
>
> The peso was at 17.5. Good or bad? This felt like something I should probably know.

**Explanatory** — open with a clear claim, establish the pattern with evidence, explain the mechanism, end with the consequence or the question that remains.
> Mexico has an unusual advantage: it sits beside the world's largest economy and already sells it almost everything. The surprising part is how little that advantage has translated into faster growth. Proximity helps. It is not, by itself, a development strategy.
>
> Nearshoring is usually described as companies moving factories to Mexico. The more important change may be what follows. A factory needs suppliers, engineers, electricity, roads, housing, and eventually competitors. The factory is the announcement. The network around it is the story.

### The earned-line test (this replaces the old aphorism ban)

A stylish sentence — an aphorism, a payoff line, a turn of phrase — ships only if it passes **both** gates.

> **Gate 1, the claim gate:** it makes a claim about this specific subject that a fact-checker could mark true or false. Mechanical check: swap the subject for another country, sector, or week. If the sentence still reads fine, it never said anything. Kill it.
>
> **Gate 2, the payoff gate:** it compresses an argument the surrounding paragraphs actually made. Delete those paragraphs and the line must die with them. A line that survives on its own is decoration, even when it is accurate. Kill it.
>
> "The factory is the announcement. The network around it is the story" dies without the supplier-and-infrastructure evidence above it, and swaps into no other week: **earned.** "Banxico blinked, the peso didn't" pastes into any week of any market newsletter: **slop.**
>
> **When in doubt, write the flat sentence. A plain fact is never slop. An unearned flourish always is.**

### Budgets (hard caps — LLM output drifts)

- **One** aphoristic payoff per piece, any surface.
- Humor per the register table (email 2, Brief/blocks 1, The Read 0, data 0).
- Questions: every question mark is paid off in the same piece — answered with evidence, or marked unresolved with what would resolve it and when. Max 2 per piece, 0 in the data layer.
- **The personification bright line:** prices, currencies, and indexes never act or feel. No "the peso shrugged", no "Banxico blinked". Institutions may act, because they do ("Banxico held its rate" is a fact). This stays a blanket ban.

### The final check (before publishing)

1. What is the most interesting fact?
2. What caused it, or what might have caused it?
3. What does it change?
4. What remains unclear?
5. Does this sound like something I would actually say?
6. Does every stylish line survive both gates?
7. Is every judgment sentence attributed?

## 4. Data-layer law (plain-factual, unchanged)

Everything that sits on a tile, table, caption, exhibit title, axis, legend, answer line, or source line. A number is the sentence. This is the old law, verbatim.

**North star:** every sentence teaches one sourced fact, with the number and its meaning in the same breath, in words you would say out loud. Reference sound: "Exports have grown by an annual average of 1.6% over the past five years, which has been a drag on overall economic growth."

1. **Lead with the claim, prove it with the number, anchor it with a baseline**, all in one sentence: "Manufacturing now makes up 22% of GDP, up from 16% in 1994."
2. **Put the number inside the main clause** ("two-way trade exceeded $623 billion"), never a floating parenthetical.
3. **One idea per sentence.** Target 12 to 25 words.
4. **Subject, verb, fact, actor first.** "Banxico held its rate." No inverted openings, no "It is worth noting that."
5. **State the fact, then its meaning, once per line.** "which has been a drag on growth." Use "which", "so", "in part because", and stop.
6. **High-frequency verbs.** Grew, fell, exceeded, averaged, remains, holds. No abstraction wearing a suit ("drove an acceleration in").
7. **Date every figure.** Source it in-line when contested.
8. **Give scale with comparison, not adjectives.**
9. **State specs flatly, like a price sheet.** "The brief covers 40 questions, updates monthly, and links every figure to a primary source."
10. **Cut the ramp.** The first sentence is the biggest fact. No "Welcome to", no restating the header.

**Data-layer before → after:**
- Number outside the clause. Before: "Mexico is a major manufacturing hub (exports: $700B)." After: "Mexico exported $700 billion of goods in 2024, most of it manufactured."
- The ramp. Before: "Migration is a complex and multifaceted phenomenon that has shaped Mexico for decades." After: "Net migration from Mexico to the US turned negative around 2009 and has stayed near zero since."
- Persuasion adjective. Before: "Remittances have grown to a staggering level." After: "Remittances hit $63 billion in 2024, more than Mexico earns from oil exports."
- Personification. Before: "The peso caught a bid and has been hovering near 17." After: "The peso traded near 17 per dollar in June 2026, up 4% for the year."

**The data-layer house paragraph (the sound of an answer):**
> Mexico's economy produced about $1.8 trillion in goods and services in 2024, the 13th largest in the world. Growth has averaged around 2% a year since 2013, held back by low investment and weak productivity. About 80% of Mexico's exports go to the United States, so US demand sets the pace for Mexican growth.

## 5. Mechanics (bind everywhere, prose and data)

**Em-dashes: never.** Both source registers use them; we do not. Swap for a period, comma, or colon. (The render also strips them, but write clean.)
- Before: "Mexico's economy — 11th largest in the world — has become manufacturing-oriented."
- After: "Mexico's economy is the 11th largest in the world. It has become increasingly oriented toward manufacturing."

**Punctuation.** Comma for a hung comparison or appositive number. Period for a new thought; when in doubt, period. Colon only to introduce a fact the first clause promised. **Semicolons: no.** Parentheses only for source-date tags: "(2024, INEGI)".

**Numbers** follow the registry in the `mexico-copy` skill: `US$723bn` (~3 sig figs, `bn` never B), `MX$315.04/day`, share `81.2%`, `17.48 MXN per US$`, big count `129m`. **Rate-level changes are percentage points: `-0.6 pp`, never `-14.5%`.**

### Ban list (delete on sight, every surface)

- The character — (em-dash) and the double hyphen doing dash work.
- Buzzwords: leverage (verb), robust, dynamic, landscape, ecosystem, headwinds, tailwinds, unlock, journey, holistic, key driver, game-changer, going forward, transformative.
- Launch-blog hype: excited to, thrilled, proud to announce, state-of-the-art, cutting-edge, best-in-class, unprecedented, revolutionize, powerful new way.
- Textbook tics: furthermore, moreover, thus, hence, notably, importantly, it should be noted, plays a crucial role, significant/substantial without a number, myriad, a range of factors, multifaceted, complex interplay.
- Empty filler: in today's world, when it comes to, the fact that, in terms of, serves as, is home to a variety of, continues to evolve, welcome to.
- Fossilized cleverness tells (the *phrases* stay banned; the underlying *move* is legal only when it passes the earned-line test): "the real story is", "here's the thing", "turns out" / "it turns out", "quietly" as an intensifier, "time will tell", "remains to be seen", "at the end of the day", "the honest number", "isn't just X, it's Y" and every not-X-but-Y reversal.
- Financial-journalese cute: whipsaw, ticks up, hovers, "the peso caught a bid", "markets shrugged", and all market/currency personification (the §3 bright line).
- Hedge spray: perhaps, potentially, arguably, in some sense. If uncertain, name the uncertainty with its shape.
- AI-imitating-a-human tells: delve, dive into, rich tapestry, vibrant, "think of it as", "let's unpack", "in a world where".
- Data-line auto-headline verbs on a generated line: because, driven by, signals (state the fact; let the reader connect it, unless the causal claim is itself sourced).
- GDP-as-wealth words: richer, wealth/wealthy for GDP per capita (use "higher GDP per person").
