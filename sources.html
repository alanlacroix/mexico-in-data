Access method — how the data comes out
APIA documented, public, machine-readable API. We pull it directly on a schedule.
BULKDownloadable files (XLS/ZIP/portal). Machine-usable, no live endpoint.
CSVDirect CSV / XML downloads, structured but manually fetched.
SDMXStatistical standard (IMF/OECD/BIS). API-like, treated as structured feed.
RSSA syndication feed we can poll for releases and headlines.
SCRAPENo feed offered; extracted from the page. Fragile, needs maintenance.
REGFree but behind registration / a key request wall.
NO FREE APIProprietary or commercial. No free first-party feed, so these are linked rather than wired.

Cadence is how often a source releases new data — daily, monthly, once a year. It's the publishing rhythm, not the freshness of any one figure. For when each wired feed last updated and what's due next, see the live status →

Who publishes this

The tables use each agency's own name. Here is what each one is and what it produces — the acronyms decoded.

Mexican government & regulators
BanxicoBanco de México
The central bank. Sets the policy rate; publishes FX, reserves, the yield curve, and remittances.
INEGIInstituto Nacional de Estadística y Geografía
The national statistics agency. Inflation, GDP, employment, and the business census.
Hacienda / SHCPSecretaría de Hacienda y Crédito Público
The finance ministry. The federal budget, spending, public debt, and tax policy.
SATServicio de Administración Tributaria
The tax authority — Mexico's IRS. Taxpayer registry and e-invoicing (CFDI) volumes.
IMSSInstituto Mexicano del Seguro Social
Social security. Its count of registered workers is the formal-jobs number.
CONEVALConsejo Nacional de Evaluación de la Política de Desarrollo Social
Measures poverty by state and municipality and grades social policy.
Secretaría de EconomíaMinistry of Economy
Trade, foreign investment (RNIE), and the DataMéxico portal.
CNBVComisión Nacional Bancaria y de Valores
The banking and securities regulator. Bank-by-bank balance sheets, credit, and capital.
COFECEComisión Federal de Competencia Económica
The antitrust regulator.
IFTInstituto Federal de Telecomunicaciones
The telecom and broadcasting regulator.
BMVBolsa Mexicana de Valores
The Mexican stock exchange.
PemexPetróleos Mexicanos
The state oil company. Crude output, exports, and the Mexican-mix (mezcla) reference price.
CFEComisión Federal de Electricidad
The state electricity utility.
CENACECentro Nacional de Control de Energía
Runs the power grid and the wholesale electricity market in real time.
CREComisión Reguladora de Energía
The energy regulator. Fuel and gas prices, permits, and tariffs.
CNHComisión Nacional de Hidrocarburos
The upstream oil-and-gas regulator. Field-level production and reserves.
SENERSecretaría de Energía
The energy ministry. National energy balances.
INEInstituto Nacional Electoral
Runs elections. The voter roll, results, and party financing.
SESNSPSecretariado Ejecutivo del Sistema Nacional de Seguridad Pública
Compiles the official crime statistics that the states report.
SegobSecretaría de Gobernación
The interior ministry. Publishes the DOF and, through the CNB, the disappeared-persons registry.
DOFDiario Oficial de la Federación
The government's official gazette: every law, decree, regulation, and tariff.

Sources we don't wire, and why
  • Proprietary, no free feed. EMBI+ Mexico, sovereign CDS, and BMV real-time quotes have no first-party free API. Each carries a NO FREE API or PROPRIETARY tag above; where the brief cites the direction of country risk, it comes from secondary press, with the date.
  • Discontinued. Google and Apple mobility ended (Google in 2022, Apple after). We keep the history and mark it as a closed series that stopped updating.
  • Endpoint unconfirmed. DataMéxico (Tesseract OLAP), CEPALSTAT, SENER, CNH, IFT, and several gob.mx / news RSS paths have live platforms but an API endpoint we haven't confirmed. Each is flagged verify endpoint above.
  • Deprecated feed. Reuters retired its public RSS; we read it via the site, not a feed.
  • Unofficial. Google Trends comes from Google, not a statistics agency. When it appears, it carries a search-interest label.
