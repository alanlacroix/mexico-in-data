About

What this is

Hi, I'm Alan Lacroix. I made this site to understand Mexico through its official numbers, and the news that moves them. Mostly the economy, politics, and the relationship with the US, though I keep finding other things to add.

It pulls data from Banxico, INEGI, Hacienda, and other original sources into one place: the peso, inflation, wages, trade, public finances, polling, crime, energy. Some of it updates daily, some once a year. Government statistics do not, unfortunately, run on my schedule.

I add the news that helps explain what's moving, and sometimes what I think it means. Having it all together has been more useful than I expected. Wages make more sense next to inflation, trade next to the peso. These numbers usually live on completely different websites.

Why I built it

I was born in Mexico City and moved to Miami when I was young. My Spanish is great. My Mexican accent, on the other hand… más o menos.

I'm building a business in Mexico now and visit often. While doing that, I realized I had lost touch with the country's social, political, and especially economic reality.

The peso was at 17.5. Good or bad? The minimum wage had gone up how much? Supermajority? How much of Mexico's economy depends on the US? These felt like things I should probably know.

I started looking for answers and found myself bouncing between ten different websites. The numbers were usually buried in government portals, databases, PDFs, and press releases. (Mexico's government produces a surprising amount of useful data. Finding it is another matter.)

The news was not helping much either. Too many sensationalized headlines in both directions, often arriving at the conclusion before giving me the information I needed to reach my own.

I wanted the facts in front of me. So I started putting them in one place.

How it works

Every figure includes its date and a link to where it came from. You can open the original source, check the number, and dig as far into the PDF as your patience allows.

I also label figures by what they are:

Official. Published directly by an institution such as Banxico, INEGI, or Hacienda.

Derived. Something I calculated from the original data. The inputs and method are there too.

Reported. A figure that comes from a news report rather than the original dataset.

Analysis or opinion. What I think the numbers mean. You are welcome to disagree. (This is true throughout the site.)

Why should you trust me? You shouldn't, at least not blindly. That is why the links and labels are there.

My name is also on the site, along with my reputation. If I get something wrong, I want to know.

How it started

I built the Brief for myself and found it extremely useful. Then I started sharing it with family and friends. They found it useful too, so I opened it up.

It's free.

The email

A few times a month, when there's something worth sending, I write an email about what changed in Mexico's economy, politics, and relationship with the US.

I look at the numbers, add the news that helps explain them, and tell you what I think. The original sources are included if you want to go deeper.

Honestly, my dad could never remember the name of the site, so sending it directly to his inbox was easier. It turns out that works well for other people too.

Things I'm interested in

Economic growth. Mexico is one of the largest economies in the world and has grown at roughly 2% for a long time. Why not faster? What would have to change?

Politics. How stable are the country's institutions? What does a supermajority actually allow the government to do? Which political changes matter outside the day's headlines?

The US–Mexico relationship. Mexico sends most of its exports to the United States. Millions of people, businesses, and families operate across the two countries. I view Mexico partly through a US lens, so I think about this one a lot.

Trade. Mexico sits next to the largest economy in the world and is its biggest trading partner. That seems like an extraordinary advantage. How much is Mexico actually making of it?

Technology and AI. Mexico has talent, proximity to the US, and a large domestic market. Will that translate into important technology companies? How will AI affect the country's services, factories, and jobs?

Capital and entrepreneurship. I'm interested in private equity, venture capital, family businesses, and the people building companies in Mexico. (I am now one of the people trying.)

Football. We always qualify, usually give ourselves hope, and still have the best jersey. We have them next time.

Tell me what I'm missing

The Brief is still a work in progress. I'm always looking for new sources, better data feeds, and clearer ways to show the information.

If a number looks wrong, something does not make sense, or you know a source I should add, I want to hear it.

Alan
