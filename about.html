About

Why I built this

My name is Alan Lacroix. I was born in Mexico City and moved to Miami when I was young. My Spanish is great; my Mexican accent, on the other hand… más o menos.

I'm building a business in Mexico now and visit often. While doing that, I realized I'd lost touch with the country's social, political, and especially economic reality.

The peso was at 17.5. Good or bad? The minimum wage had gone up how much? Supermajority? How much of Mexico's economy depends on the US?

These felt like things I should probably know.

I wanted to understand my country again. I also wanted the facts in front of me so I could come to my own conclusions.

The problem

I found myself bouncing between ten different websites just to find the original sources. The numbers were all over the place, usually buried in government portals, databases, PDFs, and press releases.

Then there was the news. Too many sensationalized headlines in both directions, often telling me what to think before giving me the information I needed to think for myself.

So I started putting everything in one place.

What the Mexico Brief is

The Brief pulls together as much current, first-party data as I can find: the official numbers, straight from the source.

The peso, inflation, wages, trade, public finances, polling, crime, energy, and whatever else I'm trying to understand.

Data isn't the whole story. It does, however, give the story a foundation. I use the news to add context and help explain what might be behind the numbers.

Putting everything together has also produced some pretty interesting insights. Wages look different next to inflation. Trade looks different next to the peso. Things that live on separate government websites can tell you a lot more when you see them side by side.

Everything is dated and linked to its source. You can check it, dig deeper, and come to your own conclusions. If something is my analysis or opinion, I tell you.

How it started

I built it for myself and found it extremely useful. Then I started sharing it with family and friends. They found it useful too, so I opened it up.

It's free.

The Brief, in your inbox

Every Monday, I send an email about what's changing in Mexico's economy, politics, and relationship with the US.

I look at the numbers, add the news that helps explain them, and tell you what I think. The original sources are there if you want to dig deeper.

Honestly, my dad could never remember the name of the site, so sending it directly to his inbox was easier. It turns out that works well for other people too.

Why should you trust me?

Don't.

At least not blindly.

Every figure links to where it came from and shows the date it's from. Mexican sources come first: Banxico, INEGI, Hacienda, and other official institutions.

I label each figure by what it is: official data, a number I derived from it, or a figure from a news report. If it's my opinion, you'll know.

You shouldn't have to take my word for any of it.

Also, my name is on the site, and so is my reputation. If I get something wrong, I want to know.

What I'm watching

I'll admit that I view the country partly through a US lens.

I'm especially interested in Mexico's political stability, economic growth, and relationship with the United States, with trade being the obvious one right now. I'm also watching AI, tech, private equity, entrepreneurship, and where Mexico positions itself in the region.

We have our problems, but I'm bullish. Mexico is one of the largest economies in the world, the biggest trading partner of the United States, and home to a national team that always qualifies just to give us hope.

We have them next time.

Tell me what I'm missing

I'm always looking for new sources, better data feeds, and clearer ways to show the information.

If a number looks wrong, something doesn't make sense, or you know a source I should add, I want to hear it.

Alan
