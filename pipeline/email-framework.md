# The Mexico Brief weekly email

This is the email I want on Monday morning.

It should tell me what changed in Mexico during the previous week, give me enough context that I do not need to open five news sites, and let me open the original source when I want more. The order stays familiar from week to week. The contents do not. Nothing appears just because a section looks empty.

There is no word-count target. The issue is as long as the useful material requires.
A short week produces a short email. Never lengthen an issue to make it feel complete,
and never retain a weaker item because the draft looks small. Stop when the reader
knows what changed, what it means, and what to watch next.

The reader is me. Keep an item only if it helps me understand Mexico, make a decision about my business there, or know what to watch this week.

The email must also be enjoyable to read. Accurate but dull is not finished. Write in
Alan's natural rhythm: direct, curious, lightly informal, and occasionally funny when
the material gives us a real opening. Vary sentence length. Explain the odd or
surprising part instead of sanding it into report language. A joke never carries a
fact, and personality never excuses imprecision. Borrow clarity and curiosity from
writers Alan likes; do not imitate their voice. The test is simple: would Alan finish
this even if it were not his newsletter?

During the final edit, read the issue for rhythm as well as accuracy. When a sentence
catches, ask what it is actually trying to say and rewrite the thought, not only the
wording. Shortening a paragraph should normally make the idea clearer too.

### Approved email voice

This is the canonical reference for the weekly email:

> I nearly wrote that Mexico added 61,023 formal jobs in June and moved on. Then I
> got to the part where platform workers accounted for 40,496 of them. The number is
> still useful, but it is a different story from 61,000 new office or factory jobs.
> This is why I keep opening the source.
>
> Also, El Niño may make groceries more expensive again. Great.

Why it works: the observation came from checking the source; the distinction is
useful; the sentences have different lengths; and the joke is short, factual in its
premise, and carries no analytical burden. Do not copy the construction every week.
Match its honesty, ease, and restraint.

## The issue

### 1. In this brief

Three to five linked lines at the top. This is navigation, not another summary.

Each line should make sense on its own and jump to the relevant section. A reader who stops here should still know what the issue covers.

### 2. A note from me

Two to five sentences, normally 80 to 140 words. Alan writes or rewrites this after the evidence is frozen.

This is the human part: what changed my view, the connection I kept coming back to, or the thing I still do not understand. It can have a joke or an aside when one comes naturally. It cannot introduce a fact that is absent from the evidence file.

Do not manufacture an opening. If I have nothing useful to add, begin with the lead.

### 3. The week

One development gets the most space. It is the event that most changed my view of Mexico that week.

Use a plain headline and connected paragraphs, not a row of labels. Cover:

- What happened.
- The relevant number or comparison.
- The context needed to understand it.
- My read, clearly labeled when it is judgment.
- What is still uncertain.
- What happens next.
- The original source and the best reporting used for context, linked beside the claim.

Aim for up to 350 words. Use less when the source supports less. If no development needs a full lead, skip this section. Do not call it a quiet week unless that is useful and true.

### 4. Also this week

Zero to three items. Each gets 90 to 140 words.

Use whatever the item needs from this list, in the order that reads naturally. Do not force every story into the same structure:

- What changed?
- Compared with what?
- What context prevents the number or headline from being misleading?
- What happens next, if known and useful?
- Where can I check it?

There are no topic quotas. Do not add a politics item because the politics section is empty. Rank everything together and keep only the strongest items.

### 5. Numbers that changed

A small, deterministic table built from the site's verified data, not generated prose.

Only include a row when a new release arrived during the issue window or a weekly market move was large enough to be useful. Do not repeat the same static board every Monday.

Each row contains:

- Metric.
- Current value.
- Change and comparison period.
- Observation period.
- Original source.
- Freshness status when the source is late or the fetch failed.

A daily market price can show a weekly move. Monthly inflation does not count as a change this week unless a new release arrived during the window.

Zero to five rows. Omit the section when nothing changed.

### 6. Other updates

Zero to four one-line updates that were worth retaining but did not earn a full item. Each line includes a direct link and date. This is not a place to empty the news database.

### 7. Next week

Up to three dated releases, votes, decisions, meetings, or deadlines.

Include the date, what it is, and one plain sentence on why it is worth watching. If the calendar is empty, omit the section.

## The chart rule

Zero or one chart per issue.

Use a chart only when it answers a specific question better than the prose. The chart should normally support the lead and should show a change, comparison, or relationship. Do not add a chart because newsletters are supposed to have visuals.

Every chart needs:

- A sentence that says how to read it.
- The observation period.
- Units.
- The original source.
- Alt text that states the main pattern without pretending the pattern proves causation.

## Subject and preview text

Use plain language.

Preferred subject:

`Mexico this week: [short, specific lead]`

Keep the full subject under 65 characters.

Preferred preview text:

`[Second development]. [The most useful number or next event].`

No question marks used as bait. No vague subjects such as "A big week for Mexico." No copied sensational headline. If the subject cannot say what happened, the lead is probably not ready.

## What earns a place

The standing beats are Mexico's economy, employment and wages, inflation and the
peso, trade and supply chains, the US-Mexico relationship, migration and population,
technology and AI, and Mexico's position between the US and China. Politics belongs
when it changes institutions, policy, investment, trade, or the ability to operate.
These are beats, not required sections. Skip a beat when nothing material changed.

Crime, deaths, and day-to-day confrontation do not belong by default. Include them
only when a development changes national policy, institutions, trade, migration, or
the operating environment enough to pass the same scoring test as every other item.

An item must first pass all of these:

1. It happened or materially changed during the issue window in Mexico City time.
2. It is specifically relevant to Mexico.
3. It is news or a new official release, not opinion, promotion, or a repeated story.
4. A primary document or credible report is available.
5. The available text supports the summary. A headline alone cannot support details.
6. Other reports about the same event are clustered into one item.

Then score it from 0 to 2 on:

- Consequence for Mexico.
- Usefulness to a decision or a change of view.
- New information this week.
- Durability beyond the news cycle.
- Evidence quality.
- Importance to the economy, politics, or the US–Mexico relationship.

A lead normally needs 10 of 12. A supporting item normally needs 7. The score creates a shortlist. Alan makes the final choice and can override it.

## Facts, analysis, and sources

Every sentence is one of these:

- **Official:** directly supported by an original institution.
- **Derived:** calculated from official inputs with the method retained.
- **Reported:** supported by a news source because an original document is unavailable.
- **Analysis:** Alan's interpretation, with the facts it rests on recorded.

Every numeral maps to retained evidence. Every source record keeps its URL, publisher, publication time, retrieval time, source type, and a hash of the fetched body. If the source cannot be fetched, the item can carry a headline and link but no generated summary.

Analysis cannot be generated and silently published. Alan approves every analytical sentence.

Link the source where the claim appears. Do not make the reader hunt through a source list at the bottom. A short source list can remain for auditability, but it is not a substitute for inline links.

## Weekly workflow

### Monday through Saturday

Collect official releases and trusted reporting. Normalize URLs, cluster duplicate coverage, retain fetched evidence, and track source health.

### Sunday

Freeze the previous Monday through Sunday window in `America/Mexico_City`. Rank eligible events and produce one draft, an evidence packet, and three questions for Alan:

1. Is this the right lead? If not, which candidate should replace it?
2. Do you have a read on the lead? One or two rough sentences are enough. "None this week" is fine.
3. What is missing, or what should be cut?

Only ask another question when a factual ambiguity blocks the draft. Do not ask Alan to rewrite the issue.

### Monday

Apply Alan's answers. Rerun the source, number, link, duplication, freshness, and voice checks. Produce the approved Beehiiv package and send a Beehiiv test.

Alan approves the delivered test before scheduling or sending. No approval means no send.

After sending, retain the approved issue, Beehiiv post URL or ID, and sent time. The permanent latest-edition link must point only to this sent record, never to Sunday's draft.

Beehiiv is the subscriber list and delivery system. Its Create Post API is currently an Enterprise-only beta, so the final paste, test, and schedule step remains manual unless the account has that access. Do not add another email provider to work around it.

## The automated 95 percent

The system can:

- Fetch and retain sources.
- Detect official releases and material market moves.
- Cluster duplicate coverage.
- Rank candidates.
- Draft factual summaries from retained text.
- Build the changed-numbers table.
- Suggest a chart when the lead would be clearer with one. Chart inclusion remains a manual editorial choice for now.
- Draft subject and preview options.
- Calculate the reading time.
- Check links, dates, units, numbers, freshness, duplication, and banned copy.
- Render desktop and mobile previews.

## The human 5 percent

Alan:

- Chooses or confirms the lead.
- Gives the issue its point of view.
- Cuts anything that does not deserve the reader's time.
- Approves analysis, subject, preview text, and the delivered test.

Alan should only need to make editorial decisions. If he has to fix broken summaries, unsupported facts, or bad routing, the draft failed.

## Send gates

Do not schedule or send unless:

- The schema is valid.
- Every factual sentence and numeral maps to retained evidence.
- No event appears twice.
- Data units, periods, revisions, and comparisons pass.
- Every analysis sentence is approved.
- All links resolve.
- The subject and preview text are plain and specific.
- The desktop and mobile previews pass.
- The delivered Beehiiv test was approved.
- The issue week has not already been sent.
- Sender authentication, reply-to, physical address, and unsubscribe behavior are verified.

## What not to do

- Do not fill sections.
- Do not send a list of everything collected.
- Do not summarize an article that was not fetched.
- Do not let fallback copy pass as a finished issue.
- Do not publish a draft as the public sample or latest edition.
- Do not use a chart as decoration.
- Do not call every item "important" or attach "why it matters" to every paragraph.
- Do not mix a new observation with an old comparison without showing both periods.
- Do not publish generated judgment under Alan's name without Alan reading it.

The email succeeds if it saves me time and leaves me with a more accurate view of Mexico. Open rate and the number of stories are secondary.
